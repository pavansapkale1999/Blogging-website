atomic bomb, also known as a fission bomb or nuclear bomb, works based on the process of nuclear fission. The fundamental principle behind an atomic bomb is to release a tremendous amount of energy by splitting the nuclei of heavy atoms, such as uranium-235 or plutonium-239. The basic design of an atomic bomb involves two subcritical masses of fissile material, which are kept apart to prevent spontaneous nuclear reactions. When the bomb is activated, conventional high explosives are used to rapidly compress these subcritical masses into a supercritical state. The detonation process can be broken down into the following steps: Implosion: The high explosives surrounding the fissile material are triggered, creating an intense and symmetric shockwave that compresses the fissile material inwards. This compression increases the density of the fissile material, bringing its nuclei closer together. Neutron Initiation: As the fissile material becomes supercritical, a significant number of neutrons are released. These neutrons initiate the chain reaction, which is the key to the atomic bomb’s explosive power. Chain Reaction: When a neutron collides with the nucleus of a fissile atom, it causes the nucleus to become unstable and split into two smaller nuclei, along with releasing additional neutrons. This process is known as nuclear fission. The released neutrons then collide with other fissile nuclei, causing them to undergo fission, and the chain reaction rapidly multiplies. Energy Release: Each fission event releases an enormous amount of energy in the form of kinetic energy and electromagnetic radiation. This energy is released almost instantaneously, resulting in a powerful explosion. Blast and Radiation: The explosion produces a massive shockwave and intense heat, causing widespread destruction in the vicinity of the blast. Additionally, the explosion releases a significant amount of ionizing radiation, which can be highly dangerous to living organisms and cause long-term health effects. The destructive power of an atomic bomb is measured in terms of its yield, which is typically expressed in kilotons (thousands of tons) or megatons (millions of tons) of TNT equivalent. The immense energy released in an atomic bomb explosion can cause devastation over a wide area and result in significant loss of life and property.

Atomic Bomb vs Hydrogen Bomb The atomic bomb and hydrogen bomb (also known as a thermonuclear bomb) are both types of nuclear weapons, but they work on different principles and have significantly different levels of destructive power. The destructive power of an atomic bomb is significant, but it is relatively less than that of a hydrogen bomb. A hydrogen bomb is a more advanced and powerful type of nuclear weapon. It operates on the principle of nuclear fusion (same as our Sun), where light atomic nuclei, such as isotopes of hydrogen (deuterium and tritium), are fused together to form heavier nuclei like helium, releasing a tremendous amount of energy. As the helium atom has much less energy than these two isotopes combined, so, the excess energy is being released. The detonation process of a hydrogen bomb involves a two-stage design. The first stage is a fission bomb, similar to an atomic bomb, which creates the high temperature and pressure needed for fusion to occur. The second stage involves the fusion of isotopes of hydrogen, producing a vastly more powerful explosion than a fission bomb. The yield of a hydrogen bomb is typically measured in megatons (millions of tons) of TNT equivalent, making it several times more powerful than an atomic bomb. The first hydrogen bomb released an energy equivalent of 10 million tons of TNT.

Story of its Discovery The story of the discovery of the nuclear bomb is intertwined with the development of nuclear physics and the events of World War II. It involved the efforts of multiple scientists and researchers from various countries. The theoretical groundwork for nuclear fission — the process that powers nuclear bombs — was laid by German physicists Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann in 1938. They discovered that when uranium atoms were bombarded with neutrons, they split into smaller nuclei, releasing a significant amount of energy. This groundbreaking discovery was later explained by Lise Meitner and Otto Frisch, who coined the term “nuclear fission” and recognized its potential for releasing an enormous amount of energy. Meanwhile, a group of scientists in the United States, led by physicist J. Robert Oppenheimer, became aware of the possibility of creating an atomic bomb using nuclear fission. The potential for building such a weapon led to the establishment of the Manhattan Project in 1942 — a top-secret research program that aimed to develop an atomic bomb during World War II. The Manhattan Project brought together some of the greatest scientific minds of the time, including Enrico Fermi, Richard Feynman, and Niels Bohr, among others. The project involved extensive research and development efforts across multiple locations, with key facilities in Los Alamos, New Mexico, and Oak Ridge, Tennessee. On July 16, 1945, the first successful test of an atomic bomb, code-named “Trinity,” took place in the desert near Alamogordo, New Mexico. The test was a success, proving the feasibility of the atomic bomb design and validating the scientific theories behind nuclear fission. A few weeks later, on August 6, 1945, the United States dropped an atomic bomb named “Little Boy” on the Japanese city of Hiroshima which released an energy equivalent to 15000 tons of TNT. The devastation caused by the bomb was immense, leading to significant loss of life and destruction of the city. Three days later, on August 9, 1945, the United States dropped a second atomic bomb, “Fat Man,” on the city of Nagasaki. The use of these two atomic bombs marked the first and, to date, the only use of nuclear weapons in warfare. “We knew the world would not be the same. A few people laughed, a few people cried.
